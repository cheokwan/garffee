Introduction:

Our Garffee project is experimenting using sound wave as a mean to facilitate indoor locationing to overcome the inaccuracy of traditional GPS-based locationing service when using indoor or underground. By deploying sources on field for emitting non-human-audible high-frequency sound wave and detecting it through the microphone of a mobile device, we can perform a beacon like location fencing mechanism or even to some extent - triangulation to pinpoint a user’s location.

Technology Details:

The threshold frequency for the non-human-audible sound wave is around 18000 Hz, which means that a normal human ear won’t be able to perceive the emitting sound if the wave frequency is higher than ~18000 Hz. The exact threshold value varies from human to human, and as the sensitivity of a human ear will degrade overtime by aging, the threshold frequency may be above or below 18000 Hz. Specifically, a child’s threshold frequency may be well into the 20000 Hz range[1]. The emitting high frequency sound wave will be unpleasant to an individual’s ear if it does not exceed the individual’s hearing threshold.

The documented iPhone microphone’s frequency response is in the range of 20 Hz to 20000 Hz[2], around the same as that of a human year. There may be some leeway at either end of the spectrum but the response is expected to drop somewhat rapidly outside of the range.

Therefore, one of the goal of the test is to determine the ideal emitting frequency that is inaudible to most audiences’ ear, can be picked up by the microphone, and still gives a reasonable un-diminished response.

Test Plan:

The test can be understood as an optimization problem with the following parameters in play:

Testing Parameters:

  1. Ideal emitting frequency

Like that discussed at the end of the Technology Details section - determine the ideal emitting frequency. Can be conducted in a controlled lab environment with samples of audience with a diverse age range; fleet of targeted mobile devices and their frequency response; and different power output of possible sources.

  1. Noise level for the device’s response

With a given target frequency, determine in various circumstances and environments the white noise level of the given frequency and intermittency of the noise spikes. So that we can come up with a target noise filter threshold level, and the magnitude and duration which corresponds to a confident detected signal. Test with different cases such as:

a. phone in a coat pocket / bag vs holding in hand

b. user walking vs standing still

c. in MTR station during busy vs quiet hours

  1. Accuracy of frequency bin for detection

Determine a reasonably small size frequency bin that can correctly identify the target frequency with a low error margin. For example, if the target frequency is 18000 Hz, a reasonably high resolution bin size that works may be +- 15 Hz. In other words, any wave detected in the 17985 Hz - 18015 Hz range over the threshold magnitude would be considered a positive signal. The bin size should not be too narrow to miss the otherwise positive matches and too wide to allow too much error. We should also consider the possibility of a moving user to address for potential doppler effect[3]. Also test for the error margin for picking up neighboring signals and refine the threshold magnitude in case we need to deploy many closely located target frequencies.

  1. Source power output

Test out different possible deployable sound emitting sources with power output constrained to its size and consumption. So that we can figure out the required deployment density to generate the signal with confidence.

  1. Source placement

Source placement is more constrained by other factors such as intrusiveness, regulations, and environment layout. The placement of the source can affect the overall quality of the emitting sound wave. Test out different placements such as:

a. closer to floor

b. closer to wall

c. closer to ceiling

d. possibility of being enclosed in non-intrusive objects such as light fixtures, advertisement boxes, garbage bins, etc.

  1. Emitting wave attenuation

Since sound wave intensity follows the inverse-square law[4], which means that the wave intensity is inversely proportional to the square of the distance apart from the source, the sound wave propagation could diminish quite significantly once left the emitting source. Determine the minimum distance required (hence affecting deployment density) between sources to maintain a good output of detectable positive signals. The function of wave intensity magnitude in relation to the distance from the source could also be used to estimate the triangulation of the device. This of course requires a field of cleverly deployed sources mapped to distinguishable frequencies and a reliable function and calculation, which probably deem too difficult with too little benefit to perform with sound wave.

  1. Demographic layout or floorplan

Targeting a location, e.g. Causeway Bay MTR, test out different demographic layouts for their effects on the wave propagation. This could affect deployment density. Scenarios that can be tested are:

        a. narrow hallway

        b. wide open area

        c. crowded area

        d. area with lots of turns and corners

        e. area with change in elevation

        f. user in train while source in station

  1. False positive environment

Test out false positive scenarios in environments where similar technology may be deployed and collision may be observed, e.g. Langham Place. Figure out the colliding frequency and avoid it.

Baseline Definition (tentative):

A confident signal is detected by receiving a continuous window of positively detected target frequency with accuracy of +-25 Hz and magnitude level greater than X dB for 5 seconds with less than 10% of noise spikes/interruptions within the duration.

Considering that an average user can exit a MTR station under 5 minutes once stepping out of the train, that is about the absolute latest time the store should starting preparing for the user’s order, hence we need to detect the presence of the user as soon as she arrives at the station.

A reasonable target deployment density would be around 20m apart between each source. The actual deployment depends on other factors such as regulation. Ideally the most dense line of defense should be deployed to the platform to capture the moment of a user stepping out of the train.
